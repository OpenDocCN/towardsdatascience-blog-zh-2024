Feeling inspired to write your first TDS post? We’re always open to contributions from new authors.

As many of our readers are getting ready to leave summer behind and return to normal work rhythms, we hope that you still carve out some time for your long-term growth—whether it’s by embarking on a side project, exploring cutting-edge ML research, or even just refreshing your data science portfolio. Regardless of where the next few months take you, we also hope TDS continues to be part of your learning journey.

From early-career advice to LLM-powered data analysis and hands-on programming tips, our most popular articles of the past month covered a lot of ground — which means that, whatever your current interests might be, you’re likely to find something here to pique your curiosity.

Here are our August must-reads — enjoy your reading!

  • My Honest Advice for Someone Who Wants to Become a Data Scientist
    If you’re just taking your very first steps towards launching a data science career and are feeling uncertain about how and where to start, Egor Howell’s candid advice—from the perspective of someone who’s a couple of years farther along the road—might just be the one post you need to read today. (It certainly resonated with a large subset of our readers!)
  • requirements.txt Is Obsolete
    “However, as versatile as the standard library is, modern Python applications often require more advanced capabilities that go beyond what is included out of the box.” Focused, actionable, and relevant for a wide cross-section of data professionals, Giorgos Myrianthous presented a well-received guide on managing Python project dependencies and metadata with Poetry.
  • ChatGPT vs. Claude vs. Gemini for Data Analysis (Part 1)
    As LLM-powered tools become more ubiquitous in day-to-day data science workflows, it’s more important than ever to develop a nuanced understanding of how they perform—and how different models stack up against each other. Yu Dong’s popular series aims to do just that, evaluating three popular options on a wide range of data-analysis tasks.
  • AI Agents — From Concepts to Practical Implementation in Python
    “Instead of prompting a single LLM to handle a complex task, we can combine multiple LLMs or AI agents, each one specializing in a specific area.” Zoumana Keita’s accessible primer makes it clear what AI agents are, why you should consider using them in real-world settings, and how to create an AI-agent system from scratch.
  • The Most Useful Advanced SQL Techniques to Succeed in the Tech Industry
    If you’ve mastered basic SQL queries and sense that it’s time to take your skills to the next level, Jiayan Yin’s practical guide is for you: from window functions to subqueries and common table expressions (CTEs), it offers concrete examples (and code snippets) to encourage you to roll up your sleeves and apply your knowledge to your own data.
  • What Nobody Tells You About RAGs
    Retrieval-augmented generation might no longer be the most buzz-generating approach to optimizing LLM outputs, but practitioners continue to discover and better understand its benefits as well as its limitations. Ahmed Besbes’s comprehensive overview unpacks the latter in detail: “getting a RAG system production-ready is about more than just stringing together some code. It’s about navigating the realities of messy data, unforeseen user queries, and the ever-present pressure to deliver tangible business value.”
  • Automating ETL to SFTP Server Using Python and SQL
    For practitioners with an interest in data-engineering and -processing topics (in other words: most of you!), Mary Ara’s new tutorial covers an essential workflow with patience and clarity: follow along to learn how you can automate the process of moving data between locations, even with the added twist of including an SFTP (secure file transfer protocol) upload.
  • The Economics of Generative AI
    “When we look back in a decade, I doubt that the companies we’ll think of as the ‘big winners’ in the generative AI business space will be the ones that actually developed the underlying tech.” To wrap things up this month, we invite you to take a few steps back and reflect on Stephanie Kirmer’s incisive analysis of the business case for generative-AI tools and the kinds of innovation it fosters — as well as those that it leaves behind.
